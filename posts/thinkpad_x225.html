X225 Laptop

I needed a laptop for traveling, so I built a laptop based on the Lenovo Thinkpad X220 and Thinkpad X230 which I'm calling the X225.

Requirements

The primary use case for the laptop will be to act as a device for typing and light web surfing whilst traveling. Therefore I have specified to following are the design requirements

  1. Must be easily portable (should fit in a 13" laptop sleeve)
  2. Must have a good keyboard
  3. Must be $500
  4. Should have a long battery life
  5. Should be repairable (with replacement parts available)
  6. Should be quiet

The Thinkpad X220

The Lenovo Thinkpad X220 laptop is a classic which is still very well loved to this day and it's easy to see why. It has a great feeling keyboard, small yet built like a tank, can be picked up on the second hand market for relatively cheap and is very modular. So it seemed like a good choice

However using a laptop which was designed 10 years ago does come with some draw backs.

  • The 2nd gen Intel Core processor runs hot
  • No USB 3.0 ports
  • The screen, whilst IPS, is only 720p

The Thinkpad X230

The Lenovo Thinkpad x230 is the next generation up from the X220 featuring USB 3.0 ports, an improved cooling design, and a 3rd gen Intel Core processor. The main differences of 3rd gen over 2nd gen is the better onboard graphics and increased efficiency which in combination with the better cooler, makes the X230 run cooler and quieter then the X220. However the X230 has the newer chiclet style keyboard which I'm not as big of a fan of compared to the older membrane keyboard which is on the X220.

Combinding the two

The Thinkpad X220 and X230 do share a one main thing which is the chassis. Therefore a common mod for the X230 is to replace the keyboard with the one from the X220. But at the time for me, there were no undamaged Thinkpad X230's on the market so I decided to do the opposite and install an X230 motherboard into a X220.

The water damaged X230

So I brought a damaged Thinkpad X230 from ebay. It was missing a hard drive and battery, the case was cracked from being dropped on one of the corners and had what looked to be some minor water damage to the screen. However the ebay listing showed it could boot so I was hopeful that the motherboard was OK.

Upon taking apart the laptop it was very clear that the laptop had serious water damage, possibly from salt water. The bottom of the case was covered of metal corrosion and some of the metal components such as the CPU heatsink mount on the motherboard had rust spots. Fortunately it still booted and everything seemed to work.

Black plastic

A quick detour but possibly one of the reasons why the water damaged motherboard still worked was because Lenovo wraps their motherboards in a layer of plastic which is glued to the motherboard with a reasonable adhesive. Unfortunately that adhesive leaves a sticky residue which can't be easily removed with isopropyl alcohol. I was able to remove most of it with a citric acid based adhesive remover but that left a orange smelling residue all over the board and the only way to remove it was to give the board a wash in warm soapy water. Electronics are fine around water right?!

Cleaning up the X220

The Thinkpad X220 which I also brought was in much better shape. The keyboard keys are a little worn and there's a couple of dings but that's to be expected of a 10 year old laptop. Lenovo applies a soft touch coating to the top of the lid and the underside of the laptop in which turns sticky as it ages but that can be easily removed using some magic eraser.

Swapping the motherboards

The main physical differences between the X220 mother board and the X230 motherboard are

  • The X230 uses a mini DisplayPort port instead of the full sized port on the X220
  • The front left USB port (next to the Display Port) is slightly longer on the X230 compared to the X220

Whilst the different sized DisplayPort ports is only a problem aesthetically (as the display port hole in the case is larger then it needs to be) longer USB port does pose a problem as it collides with a support on the bottom of the case, pushing the USB port up. The laptop will still go together however that USB port won't line up correctly with it's hole, stopping one from plugging anything into it. Plus I would prefer if the motherboard didn't have to be bent in order for it to fit.

Luckily this can be solved by filing down the supports a little until the board nicely sits in position.
